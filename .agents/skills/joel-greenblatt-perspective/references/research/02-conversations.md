## Conversation Summaries

### 1. Masters in Business — Barry Ritholtz (Bloomberg, October 2020)

**URL:** https://ritholtz.com/2020/10/transcript-joel-greenblatt-2/
**Credibility:** ★★★★★ Primary (verbatim transcript, 12,000+ words)
**Format:** Extended one-on-one interview

**Context:** Greenblatt was promoting his book *Common Sense: The Investor's Guide to Equality, Opportunity, and Growth*. The interview ranges from his personal investing history to policy proposals.

**Key themes:**

- **Gotham Capital origins:** Started with $7M from Michael Milken. Focused on special situations. 50% gross / ~30% net annualized returns for a decade.
- **Closed to outside investors in 1995:** Returned ~$500M to partners. Ran only family office money until 2009.
- **Reopened in 2009** with mutual funds — a deliberate shift to a more scalable, diversified strategy.
- **Michael Burry relationship:** Discovered Burry's online posts on Silicon Investor. Backed Burry's first fund. "He was brilliant." Gotham invested $100M with Scion by 2006.
- **Magic Formula:** Designed to give individual investors "a fighting chance." Not meant to be optimal — meant to be simple enough to stick with.
- **On market efficiency:** "Prices fluctuate more than values. That's the secret." Elaborated with the "20 bills on the sidewalk" analogy — opportunities keep appearing, you just have to bend down.
- **On active vs passive:** Most people should be passive. But active managers who can value businesses can exploit the inefficiencies created by passive flows.
- **On political economy (*Common Sense*):** Argues for a "pro-growth, pro-equality, pro-opportunity" agenda. Criticizes both sides of the aisle. Advocates for immigration reform, education reform, and tax code simplification.

**Notable impromptu moments:**
- When Ritholtz pressed him on why he didn't open a hedge fund again, he laughed and said managing $7B in mutual funds was "interesting enough" and he didn't want the complexity.
- Spontaneously pivoted to discussing Success Academy charter schools (he's a co-founder) — clearly his passion project outside investing.
- His voice changed noticeably when discussing Burry — genuine admiration and pride in having been an early supporter.

### 2. Investor's Chronicle Podcast — Simon Thompson (2023)

**URL:** https://shows.acast.com/investorschronicle/episodes/ic-interviews-joel-greenblatt
**Partial transcript via:** https://stockspinoffinvesting.com/joel-greenblatt-podcast-interview
**Credibility:** ★★★★☆ Primary (partial transcript, blog summary)

**Key content — Spin-off analogy (his most detailed impromptu explanation):**

> "Suppose there was a law that said that every time you bought a house in New York you also had to buy one in Cleveland. And every time you bought a house in Cleveland, you also had to buy one in New York. The combined value of those two houses would be one price. Now assume that the law was suddenly repealed. Now, the highest and best buyer for the house in New York could just buy the house in New York. And the highest and best buyer in Cleveland could buy the house in Cleveland. The combined value of those two houses would go up. Because you aren't forced to buy something that you don't want."

**Additional spin-off insights:**
- Management usually doesn't like cutting their empire — spin-offs are "selfless" acts that signal management is thinking of shareholders.
- Unlike IPOs, spin-offs have no roadshow hype. The new company is "not hyped, maybe undervalued or misvalued or not even followed."
- "They could be overpriced too because they aren't followed well. They could be underpriced or overpriced. I would just say it is a fertile ground to look for opportunity."
- Notes that original shareholders of the parent often sell the spun-off entity indiscriminately, creating entry opportunities.

## Thematic Threads (Cross-Cutting)

### Theme 1: The Centrality of Valuation

Every single conversation converges on this: **valuation is the only sustainable edge.** Greenblatt consistently rejects style labels ("value," "growth") in favor of the verb "valuing." From the Masters in Business interview to Columbia lectures to podcast appearances, this is his bedrock principle.

### Theme 2: Behavior Over Intellect

Greenblatt repeatedly emphasizes that intelligence is not the differentiator. In the Wisdom, Wealth & Wellness interview, he explicitly states that knowledge reduces emotional vulnerability. In the Money Maze podcast, he admits he would have been fired if not for owning his firm. The Magic Formula is explicitly designed to *force* good behavior rather than rely on the investor's willpower.

### Theme 3: Special Situations as Natural Advantage

Despite building an institutional quant platform, Greenblatt's heart remains in special situations. The spin-off analogy (NY/Cleveland houses) is his most vivid and impromptu metaphor. He still believes spin-offs are "fertile ground" — even though his day-to-day work now involves 300+ stock portfolios and quantitative screens.

### Theme 4: The Burry Connection

Greenblatt has told the Burry story across multiple interviews with consistent detail and evident pride. He recognized Burry's talent from online posts, invested $1M in seed capital, and watched Scion grow to $100M of Gotham's capital. This is one of his most referenced "pattern recognition" stories.

### Theme 5: Active vs Passive — The Pragmatic Split

Greenblatt is notably pragmatic: passive is correct for most people. But he argues that if everyone goes passive, the market becomes *more* inefficient, creating opportunities for active managers who can value businesses. This is not an ideological position but a structural one.

## Contradictions & Evolution of Views

### Contradiction 1: Concentrated vs Diversified

| Then (1985–1994) | Now (2009–present) |
|------------------|-------------------|
| Gotham Capital: highly concentrated special situations, very few positions | Gotham Asset Management: 300–400 longs + 300–400 shorts |
| Bet big on best ideas | Diversification actually *improves* returns in their quantitative framework |
| "You Can Be a Stock Market Genius" celebrates concentrated bets | Research showed diversified portfolios outperformed concentrated for their systematic approach |

**Reconciliation:** His *approach* changed because his *scale* and *methodology* changed. Special situations (spin-offs, restructurings) are inherently limited in capacity. The quant system benefits from breadth. He hasn't repudiated concentration for individual investors who can genuinely identify special situations.

### Contradiction 2: Active vs Passive

| Active side | Passive side |
|-------------|-------------|
| Runs an active quant fund with 600+ positions | "Most people should be passive" |
| Believes active managers can exploit market inefficiencies | Acknowledges most active managers underperform |
| Argues passive flows create pricing distortions | Says passive is the right choice for those who can't value businesses |

**Reconciliation:** Not a contradiction — a division of labor. He is explicit: if you can't or won't value businesses, buy the index. If you can, there are opportunities. This is internally consistent.

### Contradiction 3: The Magic Formula's Audience

| Book claim | Practical reality |
|------------|------------------|
| "Anyone can do this" (Little Book) | Requires discipline most people lack |
| Simple formula, 30 stocks | His own firm uses far more complex, multi-factor models |
| Works over the long term | High short-term volatility causes abandonment |

**Reconciliation:** The Magic Formula is a teaching device, not his actual strategy. It simplifies complex principles into an actionable system. He has acknowledged in multiple interviews that most people cannot stick with it — but that's a failure of behavior, not of the formula.

### Evolution: From Event-Driven to Systematic

The clearest arc across all conversations:

1. **1985–1994:** Pure special situations. High-touch, concentrated, labor-intensive. 50% returns.
2. **1995–2009:** Family office. Continued research. Developed systematic screens.
3. **2009–present:** Diversified quant platform. 300+ longs/shorts. Lower returns but scalable and consistent.

This is not a contradiction — it's adaptation to scale. Greenblatt is unusual in that he *voluntarily* reduced his return target to build a more durable, scalable business.

## Notable Omissions & Gaps

1. **Views on specific holdings:** Greenblatt rarely discusses individual portfolio positions in interviews. His 13F filings (NVDA, AAPL, AMZN, SNOW as of early 2026) are the only window into current holdings.

2. **Magic Formula performance since publication:** He has not publicly updated the backtest or provided a track record of the public website's results.

3. **Relationship with other value investors:** Beyond Burry, he has said little about Klarman, Buffett, or other peers. His Columbia class occasionally featured guest speakers (Richard Pzena appears in class notes).

4. **Views on crypto / modern market structure:** Not addressed in any of the sources found.

5. **Investment failures:** He discusses mistakes generally but has not done a "post-mortem" of any specific failed investment outside the brief "most disastrous investment" clip in the Columbia playlist.

## Summary Assessment

Greenblatt's thinking, across all available conversations, is remarkably consistent on fundamentals and pragmatically adaptive on implementation. His core principles have not shifted since the 1980s:
- Value businesses, don't just label them
- Price and value diverge in the short run
- Behavior matters more than intelligence
- Special situations offer structural advantages

What *has* evolved is his implementation: from concentrated special-situations hunter to diversified quantitative manager. This evolution is driven by scale and self-awareness, not a change in philosophy.

His most distinctive trait across all conversations is **intellectual honesty**: he admits what he doesn't know, acknowledges when his approach changed (and why), and designs systems (the Magic Formula, his quant platform) that account for human weakness rather than relying on willpower.
