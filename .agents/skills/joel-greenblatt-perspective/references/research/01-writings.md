## 1. BOOKS PUBLISHED

### 1.1 *You Can Be a Stock Market Genius: Uncover the Secret Hiding Places of Stock Market Profits* (1997)

**Published:** Simon & Schuster, February 25, 1997 (ISBN 978-0684840079, 304 pp)
**Grade:** ⬤

**Core topic:** Special-situation / catalyst-driven value investing. Greenblatt's first book, written after Gotham Capital had already compounded at ~40% annually (1985–1994).

**Core arguments (5–10):**

1. **Special situations are systematically underpriced** because institutional investors are forced to sell. Spin-offs, restructurings, mergers, bankruptcies, rights offerings, recapitalizations — these create mechanical selling pressure that has nothing to do with fundamentals. The individual investor can exploit this. (⬤ — per publisher summary and class notes)

2. **You don't need to be a genius; you need to go where others aren't looking.** "My goal is to teach you the course that I never had... We think simply and a little bit differently." Most smart people on Wall Street fail because they compete in efficient areas; the edge is in ignored corners. (⬤ — Greenblatt Class #1, Sept 7, 2005)

3. **Spin-offs are the most reliable special situation.** Post-spin-off, institutional index funds and style-box investors dump the new entity regardless of price, creating a structural mispricing window that can last weeks to months. Greenblatt documented 30%+ annualized returns in selected spinoff situations. (⬤ — *You Can Be a Stock Market Genius*; corroborated by invest-like.com analysis)

4. **Complex capital structures are a feature, not a bug.** The more purposely complicated a situation looks, the higher the chance of mispricing. Example: Liberty Media's deliberately opaque spin-off structure, where the CEO was incentivized to depress the initial trading price to maximize his option grant. (⬤ — class case study per stockspinoffinvesting.com)

5. **Catalyst matters, but time is the ultimate catalyst.** "I would love to have a catalyst on everything I do. Eventually, in three years or more you don't even need a catalyst." The efficient market hypothesis is "right but only long term." (⬤ — Class #2 notes, focusedcompounding.com)

6. **Concentration is safer than diversification when you truly know your companies.** Gotham invested in 5–8 securities. "Why that is more safe than diversifying? You pick your spots." A concentrated portfolio of 8 stocks has historically had only 2% higher standard deviation than a fully diversified one. (⬤ — Class #1 notes; also in book)

7. **Independent research trumps analyst opinions.** Analysts are incentivized to issue buy recommendations (access, commissions, investment banking relationships). "You can't trust analysts to provide you with impartial recommendations." (⬤ — book; per shortform.com summary)

8. **Small caps are inherently more inefficient.** "Focus on small caps where the markets are more inefficient. There is less analyst coverage so less information flow. You have the chance to find prices more above or below value." (⬤ — Class #1 notes)

9. **Avoid activist investing.** Greenblatt won a proxy fight and made money, but called it "his first and last foray into activist investing" — not worth the pain. (⬤ — Class #1 notes)

10. **The Marriott spin-off as canonical case.** Marriott Corp split into Marriott International (management business) and Host Marriott (debt-laden real estate). Institutional sellers dumped Host Marriott shares because they were too small and too indebted — creating a sub-NAV pricing window Greenblatt exploited. (⬤ — book; invest-like.com corroboration)

### 1.2 *The Little Book That Beats the Market* (2005)

**Published:** John Wiley & Sons, 2005 (ISBN 978-0471733065, 155 pp). Updated as *The Little Book That Still Beats the Market* (2010, ISBN 978-0470624159).
**Grade:** ⬤

**Core topic:** The Magic Formula — a mechanical two-factor value investing system for retail investors.

**Core arguments (5–10):**

1. **The Magic Formula: rank by earnings yield (cheapness) AND return on capital (quality), then combine the ranks.** Buy the 20–30 stocks with the best combined score. (⬤ — book; magicformulainvesting.com)

2. **"Buying above-average companies at below-average prices" is the entire game.** The formula operationalizes Warren Buffett's insight: quality matters, not just cheapness (which was pure Graham). This combination is why the formula works when simple low-P/E strategies also work but less consistently. (⬤ — Forbes interview with Greenblatt, Jan 2010)

3. **Use EBIT/Enterprise Value for earnings yield, not P/E.** EBIT strips out tax and capital-structure differences. Enterprise Value (market cap + debt − cash) captures the true cost of buying the business. This allows apples-to-apples comparison across companies. (⬤ — Forbes briefing book, Jan 2010)

4. **Use EBIT/(Net Working Capital + Net Fixed Assets) for return on capital, not ROE.** ROE is distorted by leverage. ROC measures how efficiently the business itself turns invested capital into earnings, independent of financing. "Do I care about the ROE? I care about the return on capital (ROIC)." (⬤ — Class #1 notes; Forbes briefing)

5. **The magic formula works because the principles behind it are timeless.** "In order for the magic formula to make us money in the long run, the principles behind it must appear not only sensible and logical, but timeless. Otherwise, there is no way we will be able to 'hang on' when our short-term results turn against us." (⬤ — Class notes, focusedcompounding.com)

6. **The formula requires faith and patience.** It fails to beat the market 5 out of every 12 months, and about 1 in 4 years. But over 3-year rolling periods it beats the market 95% of the time (160 out of 169 periods tested). The worst 3-year return was +11% vs. the market's −46%. (⬤ — book appendix; class notes)

7. **Most investors fail not because the formula is wrong but because they can't stick with it during drawdowns.** The biggest barrier is psychological, not analytical. "It will be your belief in the overwhelming logic of the magic formula that will make the formula work for you in the long run." (⬤ — book)

8. **No size effect: the formula works for large companies too.** Backtest of top 1,000 largest companies: 22.9% CAGR vs. 12.4% for the S&P 500 over 17 years. (⬤ — class notes)

9. **Exclude financials and utilities.** EBIT/EV and ROC calculations don't make sense for banks (where "interest" is core business) or regulated utilities. Greenblatt later developed separate methods for these sectors but hasn't shared them widely. (⬤ — GuruFocus interview)

10. **$11,000 turns into $1 million before taxes.** The original backtest (1988–2004): 30.8% CAGR for a 30-stock portfolio vs. 12.4% for the S&P 500. The 2010 update extended through the 2008 crisis with still-positive results but reduced magnitude. (⬤ — book appendix; Wikipedia)

### 1.3 *The Big Secret for the Small Investor* (2011)

**Published:** Random House / Crown Business, April 2011 (ISBN 978-0307720788, ~205 pp)
**Grade:** ⬤

**Core topic:** Why individual investors have structural advantages over institutional managers, and how to exploit them through alternative index weighting.

**Core arguments (5–10):**

1. **The "big secret": individual investors can beat the market, index funds, AND professional managers.** The secret is not stock-picking skill but structural advantages: no clients, no career risk, no short-term performance pressure, ability to hold for decades, ability to invest in small caps. (⬤ — book synopsis, PenguinRandomHouse)

2. **Capitalization-weighted index funds are fundamentally flawed.** They force you to buy more of a stock *after* its price has risen (because its weight in the index increases). This is backward: you should buy more when it's cheap, not when it's expensive. (⬤ — book; novelinvestor.com summary)

3. **Equal-weighted index funds beat cap-weighted funds over the long term.** By ~1.5–2% annually over the prior two decades before fees. But equal-weighted funds have higher turnover and trading costs. (⬤ — book; theinvestorhandbook.com)

4. **Fundamentally-weighted index funds beat both.** Instead of weighting by market cap, weight by fundamental metrics (earnings, dividends, book value). This captures the value premium systematically without relying on a manager's skill. (⬤ — book)

5. **Value-weighted index funds are the optimal solution.** Combining fundamental weighting with a value tilt (buying cheap companies within the index) should outperform all other passive approaches. Greenblatt's backtest: value-weighted index returned 13.9% annually (1990–2010) vs. 7.6% for cap-weighted S&P 500. (⬤ — book; theinvestorhandbook.com)

6. **Most mutual fund managers are structurally shut out from their best opportunities.** Regulations limit how much they can own of any company (often max 5%). A tiny position in a future Microsoft is not worth their analysis time. This creates an uncompetitive arena for small investors. (⬤ — book; theinvestorhandbook.com)

7. **Picking good fund managers is nearly impossible.** Investors fire managers after poor performance and hire after good performance — but fired managers tend to outperform hired managers going forward. The timing penalty destroys any fund-picking advantage. (⬤ — book; novelinvestor.com)

8. **The individual investor's true advantage: time horizon.** "Investing with the managers who have performed the best and attracted all the money is probably a great way to win the last war, just not a great strategy for beating the market going forward." (⬤ — book)

9. **Compare earnings yield to the risk-free rate (10-year Treasury).** If risk-free rate is below 6%, Greenblatt uses 6% as a conservative minimum discount rate. Ask: is the earnings yield of this investment significantly higher than the risk-free alternative? (⬤ — book; novelinvestor.com)

10. **Three sources of edge for small investors: small caps, concentration, special situations.** "How do you compete against the brains on Wall Street? Play a different game." (⬤ — book)

### 1.4 *Common Sense: The Investor's Guide to Equality, Opportunity, and Growth* (2020)

**Published:** Columbia University Press, September 2020 (ISBN 978-0231198905, 144 pp)
**Grade:** ⬤

**Core topic:** Public policy proposals from a value-investor perspective — education, immigration, banking regulation, retirement savings. NOT an investing book.

**Core arguments (5–10):**

1. **The U.S. education system is designed to be unequal by zip code.** Only 1 in 11 black, Hispanic, or low-income students in top 50 U.S. cities makes it to college. The current trillion-dollar system fails 10 out of 11 students. (⬤ — book; ValueWalk interview)

2. **Charter schools (like Success Academy, which Greenblatt co-founded) can close the achievement gap.** His network of 46 schools serving 20,000 students outperforms every school district in New York state on combined math and English scores. (⬤ — book; Gabelli School lecture, Sept 2020)

3. **"Alternative certification" can disrupt the college-degree monopoly.** Large companies (Google, Microsoft, Amazon) should create their own tests/certificates that serve as substitutes for college degrees in hiring. This would create a new low-cost ecosystem for job qualification. "Set your own standards for what tests or courses they will look at." (⬤ — book; ValueWalk interview)

4. **Expanded Earned Income Tax Credit can guarantee a $15/hour minimum wage without the negative employment effects of a mandated minimum wage.** Would ultimately cost "less than free" by reducing poverty-driven social costs. (⬤ — book; WealthTrack interview)

5. **Skilled immigration is a "free goldmine."** For every skilled immigrant admitted, the U.S. collects $500K–$1M in net tax revenue and creates 183 additional jobs for every 100 skilled foreign-born workers. Immigrants founded 51% of U.S. billion-dollar startups and 216 of the Fortune 500. (⬤ — book; WealthTrack interview)

6. **Bank capital requirements should be raised to 20–30% of total assets**, using a new tax-advantaged preferred stock structure (dividends tax-deductible for banks, tax-free for investors) to eliminate "too big to fail." This simplifies regulation and aligns incentives. (⬤ — book; sobrief.com)

7. **Adopt Australia's superannuation system for U.S. retirement.** Require employers to contribute ~9.5% of wages to personal retirement accounts. Divert a portion of Social Security taxes into these accounts. Nearly half of working-age U.S. families have zero retirement savings. (⬤ — book; WealthTrack interview)

8. **"Capitalism only works when everyone has a fair chance."** Greenblatt frames the book as an investor's diagnosis of systemic problems: identify misallocations of capital (human, educational, financial) and propose marginal changes with high return-on-investment. (⬤ — WealthTrack interview, Aug 2020)

9. **Named after Thomas Paine's "Common Sense"** — the approach is to question foundational assumptions ("Who is this king guy and who picked him?") rather than arguing about marginal fixes. (⬤ — Gabelli School lecture)

10. **Criticism noted:** Readers found the policy proposals "center-right business-oriented solutions," overly complicated, and "straw man" arguments. Greenblatt "shines as an investor but doesn't quite hit the mark as a public policy writer." (○ — nateshivar.com book review; note this is a contrary view)

## 2. GOTHAM CAPITAL INVESTMENT METHODOLOGY

**Grade:** ⬤ (Greenblatt's own accounts) + ◐ (third-party analysis)

**Timeline and performance:**
- Founded 1985 with $7M, seeded largely by Michael Milken
- Partner Robert Goldstein joined 1989
- 1985–1994: ~40% annualized returns before fees; ~30% net of fees (⬤ — Wikipedia; invest-like.com)
- By January 1995, returned ALL outside capital (~$500M) because the fund's asset base had outgrown the special-situation opportunity set (⬤ — Class #1 notes; invest-like.com)
- Greenblatt continued managing his own money; co-founded Gotham Asset Management later, running long-short mutual funds

**Methodology:**

1. **Concentrated special-situation investing with a catalyst.** "We invest in 5 to 8 securities. Know your companies very well." (⬤ — Class #1)

2. **Identify forced selling.** The core insight: institutions are often compelled to sell for non-fundamental reasons (index reconstitution, style-box drift, minimum position size, mandate restrictions). These create pricing dislocations. (⬤ — book; class notes)

3. **Deep value with a margin of safety.** Greenblatt's consistent framing: "Figure out the value of a company or security and then try to buy it at a big enough discount so that we are left with a large margin of safety." (⬤ — GuruFocus interview)

4. **Stayed small to preserve the edge.** Returning outside capital was the key strategic decision. "Gotham Capital stayed small. We returned outside capital, so we could invest in as many situations as possible (not constrained by size)." (⬤ — Class #1)

5. **Normalized earnings are the art.** The screen is mechanical, but the valuation work is discretionary. "Use normalized EBIT. Look at the normal environment. This is the art part." (⬤ — Class #2)

6. **"I don't predict under or out performance of the value cycle."** Greenblatt explicitly disclaims market timing. He buys what's cheap regardless of macro. (⬤ — Class #2)

## 3. COLUMBIA BUSINESS SCHOOL TEACHING

**Course:** "Value and Special Situation Investing" (since 1996)
**Grade:** ⬤ (his own lectures) + ◐ (student notes, ThematicEdge series)

**Key teaching themes:**

1. **"Prices fluctuate more than values — so therein lies opportunity."** The single most important framing principle. (⬤ — Class #1; thematicedge.substack.com)

2. **"If you do good valuation work and you are right, Mr. Market will pay you back. In the short term, one to two years, the market is inefficient. But in the long-term, the market has to get it right — it will pay you back in two to three years."** (⬤ — Class #1)

3. **Four ways to value a company:** (a) DCF / intrinsic value, (b) relative value, (c) break-up value, (d) acquisition value. "Replicating value — I don't usually do that. The communists made square wheels because they cost the same to make as round wheels." (⬤ — Class notes)

4. **"Value investing is not just buying low P/E multiples. In value investing, you are buying something at a discount to what you think it is worth."** (⬤ — Class #4)

5. **"I look for investments where I think I'll make money, but if I'm wrong, I won't lose much — because I have such a margin of safety."** (⬤ — Class #4)

6. **"You can't use leverage — because you need to live through downturns. That's incredibly important."** (⬤ — Class #4)

7. **"Limit yourself to the companies where you can figure out normalized earnings. I only invest in companies where I can figure that out — or where it's easy to."** (⬤ — Class #4)

8. **The Value Investors Club (VIC) as a teaching tool.** Greenblatt co-founded VIC (valueinvestorsclub.com) with John Petry. It's a private forum where approved members post detailed write-ups. He requires students to study it. "I can't emphasize enough my recommendation to study the Value Investor's Club because you can obtain more experience and learn from other's mistakes." (⬤ — Class #2)

9. **Guest lectures from practitioners:** Richard Pzena (Pzena Investment Management), Brian Gaines (Gotham analyst), Greenblatt's sister Linda (consumer/retail investing). The class mixes theory with real-world case studies from situations Greenblatt himself was working on. (⬤ — class notes)

10. **Teaching sharpens his own framework.** Greenblatt continues teaching despite managing billions because "the questions students raise force him to re-examine assumptions he might otherwise leave unchallenged." (◐ — invest-like.com; multiple interviews)

## 6. COINED TERMS AND CONCEPTS

| Term | Definition | Source | Notes |
|------|-----------|--------|-------|
| **Magic Formula** | A two-factor stock ranking system: rank by earnings yield + return on capital, sum the ranks, buy top 20–30 | *Little Book* (2005) | Greenblatt's most famous concept |
| **Special Situation** | A corporate event (spin-off, restructuring, bankruptcy, merger, rights offering) that creates a temporary pricing dislocation | *Stock Market Genius* (1997) | Borrowed from Graham/Buffett but Greenblatt systematized |
| **Earnings Yield** | EBIT / Enterprise Value — used instead of P/E for cross-company comparison | *Little Book* (2005); Class #2 | Greenblatt popularized EV-based earnings yield for retail |
| **Return on Capital (ROC)** | EBIT / (Net Working Capital + Net Fixed Assets) — preferred over ROE | *Little Book* (2005); Class #1 | Measures business quality independent of leverage |
| **Value-Weighted Index** | An index where stocks are weighted by fundamental metrics (earnings, book value, dividends) instead of market cap | *Big Secret* (2011) | Greenblatt's proposed improvement over cap-weighted indexing |
| **Alternative Certification** | Employer-created test/course standards replacing college degree requirements for hiring | *Common Sense* (2020) | Policy proposal, not investing |
| **"What You Pay / What You Earn"** | Framing for the two Magic Formula ratios: What You Pay = EBIT/EV; What You Earn = EBIT/IC | Class #2 (2005) | Simple pedagogical framing |
| **"The Art Part"** | Determining normalized EBIT — the discretionary judgment that separates mechanical screening from real investing | Class #2 (2005) | Repeated in interviews |

## 8. CONTRADICTIONS & TENSIONS (Recorded without Harmonization)

1. **Concentration vs. Diversification:** Greenblatt says "invest in 5–8 securities" (*Stock Market Genius*, Class #1) and ran Gotham that way. But the Magic Formula prescribes 20–30 stocks. He resolves this by saying "neither method is better than the other... both can work great" (GuruFocus), but the tension between deep concentration and systematic diversification is never fully resolved.

2. **Mechanical vs. Discretionary:** The Magic Formula is presented as a mechanical screen anyone can run. But Greenblatt repeatedly says "the art part" of normalizing earnings is critical, and that he can't value most companies. If the formula requires judgment to work well, it's not truly mechanical — which undermines the core selling point of the book.

3. **"The formula works even after everyone knows it" vs. "If everyone used it, it would probably stop working."** Greenblatt says both things. The first in the book's premise; the second in Class #2 ("If the magic formula worked all the time, everyone would use it. If everyone used it, it would probably stop working."). He never fully explains how both can be true simultaneously, except to note that most people lack the discipline to stick with it.

4. **Anti-index fund stance vs. proposing a better index fund.** *Big Secret* criticizes cap-weighted indexing as structurally flawed, but then proposes value-weighted indexing — which is still a form of passive indexing. The critique is of the *weighting scheme*, not index funds per se, but the marketing framed it as contrarian.

5. **Small-cap advantage vs. Magic Formula database.** The Magic Formula screens only top ~3,500 U.S. stocks by market cap (excluding those below $50M). But Greenblatt's earlier success came from micro-cap special situations. The formula excludes the very universe where his own edge was largest.

6. **Time horizon tension in teaching.** Greenblatt tells students to be patient (3–5 year holding periods) but the Magic Formula rebalances annually. If you sell after one year regardless, you're not giving Mr. Market the full 2–3 years Greenblatt says is needed.

7. **Activist investing rejection vs. special-situations logic.** Greenblatt won a proxy fight, made money, and swore never to do it again ("not worth the pain"). Yet many special situations benefit from an activist catalyst to close the value gap. Greenblatt wants to free-ride on other activists' work rather than doing it himself.

8. ***Common Sense* is not an investing book** — yet it's marketed as "The Investor's Guide to Equality, Opportunity, and Growth." Several readers expressed disappointment that it was a policy book, not an investing book. Greenblatt explicitly says he wrote it "as a long-term investor" bringing a different perspective, but the branding mismatch is notable.
