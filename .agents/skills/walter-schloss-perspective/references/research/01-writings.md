## 1. Primary Sources (Written by Walter Schloss)

These are pieces **authored directly by Walter J. Schloss**. All are HIGH credibility.

### 1.1 "Benjamin Graham and Security Analysis: A Reminiscence" (1976/1980s)

- **Type**: First-person essay / memorial reflection
- **Date**: Written sometime between 1976 (Graham's death) and the 1980s; published in *The Rediscovered Ben Graham*
- **Status**: HIGH — Primary source, Schloss's own words
- **Source URL**: http://www.grahamanddoddsville.net/wordpress/Files/Gurus/Benjamin%20Graham/Graham%20-%20A%20Reminiscence%20-%20Schloss.pdf
- **Transcript**: Full text available at https://acquirersmultiple.com/2016/08/benjamin-graham-and-security-analysis-a-reminiscence-walter-j-schloss/

**Key contents**:
- Recounts how partner Armand Erpf told him in 1935: "Read the book [Security Analysis] and when you know everything in it, you won't have to read anything else."
- Took Graham's Advanced Security Analysis course at the New York Stock Exchange Institute
- Graham offered him a job as security analyst as he was leaving the Army at end of 1945: "He changed my life."
- Recounts the GEICO purchase: Graham told him "Walter, if this purchase doesn't work out, we can always liquidate it and get our money back."
- Helped with the third edition of *Security Analysis* (1951) — wrote the appendix on special situations
- Graham-Newman's total net assets on January 31, 1946: $3,300,000
- "Ben's emphasis was on protecting his expectation of profit with minimum risk."
- "Fear and greed tend to affect one's judgment. Because Ben was not really very aggressive about making money, he was less affected by these emotions than were many others."
- "Ben had been hurt by the Depression, so he wanted to invest in things that would protect him on the downside."
- Graham wrote that analysts should not use more than arithmetic and "possibly a little algebra"

### 1.2 "Three Industrial Stock Averages Contrasted" — Co-authored with Benjamin Graham

- **Type**: Academic article, co-authored by Schloss and Graham
- **Date**: Undated, but likely 1940s–1950s during Schloss's tenure at Graham-Newman
- **Status**: HIGH — Primary source, co-authored
- **Source URL**: https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/5b7c186c36ef662dc0a4b05a/67893fe3fd0de31fe739838f_Three%20Industrial%20Stock%20Averages%20Contrasted.pdf
- **Description**: "Benjamin Graham and Walter Schloss consider closely the relative movement of three representative indexes of industrial common stocks, to note their divergences, and to seek the reasons therefor."

This is notable: it is the ONLY known piece of formal financial literature that Schloss co-authored with Benjamin Graham. It demonstrates that Graham trusted Schloss as a co-author and that Schloss had genuine analytical writing skills.

These are transcripts or records of Schloss speaking. They represent his OWN words (HIGH) but filtered through an interviewer/transcriber (MEDIUM-HIGH).

### 2.1 Outstanding Investor Digest (OID) Interview — March 6, 1989 (Part I)

- **Type**: In-depth interview transcript
- **Date**: March 6, 1989
- **Status**: HIGH — Quoted verbatim transcript from a respected publication
- **Source URL**: https://hedgefundalpha.com/strategies/walter-schloss-interview-oid/

- **Also hosted at**: http://csinvesting.org/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/Walter-Schloss-OID-Interview.pdf

**Key excerpts**:

**On Graham's purchase of GEICO**:
> "When Graham-Newman bought it, GEICO was ready to take off but they didn't know it. Nobody recognized that their gradual growth was about to accelerate. It was viewed as just a nice little company making money."

**On his first ten years**:
> "I think the first ten years you get kind of acquainted with what you're doing."

**On performance**:
- For the 33 years ended 12/31/88: compound annual return of **21.6%** per year on equity capital vs. **9.8%** per year for the S&P 500.

**On non-conformism** (via the story of Mr. Warburg):
> "Warburg was a non-conformist." / Edwin Schloss: "Thankfully for him and his family, he was a contrarian."

**On children of successful fathers**:
> "I've noticed that children of very successful fathers quite often don't get along with their fathers and leave. But in many cases where the sons and the fathers do get along, the sons do much better than the fathers."

**On compound interest**:
> "If you start with a dollar and you double it every so many years, it builds up. In the first twenty years it doesn't look like much but eventually it does."

### 2.2 Outstanding Investor Digest Interview — June 23, 1989 (Part II)

- **Type**: Continuation of the OID interview
- **Date**: June 23, 1989
- **Status**: HIGH — Verbatim transcript
- **Source URL**: https://theoraclesclassroom.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/12/1989-6-Mar-IV-2.pdf
- **Referenced in**: Farnam Street newsletter

**Key excerpts (from secondary references)**:

**On selling discipline**:
> "I guess because it's selling at a price we think is reasonable. Usually what happens is that when a company's earnings are getting better, its value goes higher; and it's somewhat difficult for us to adjust to the new facts. If we buy a stock at $30 and believe it's worth $50, once it gets up to $50, we may believe it's really worth $60. But it's hard to adjust to the new circumstances. So there's a tendency to sell too soon."

**On the simplicity of their approach**:
> "We're buying in a way that we don't have to be too smart about the business."

**On the Buffett golf story** (Schloss recounting a story about Buffett):
> "Warren (Buffett) was playing golf at Pebble Beach with Charlie Munger, Jack Byrne, and another person. One of them proposed: 'Warren, if you shoot a hole-in-one on this 18-hole course, we'll give you $10,000. If you don't shoot a hole-in-one, you owe us $10.' Warren thought about it and said, 'I'm not taking the bet.' The others said, 'Why don't you? The most you can lose is $10. You can make $10,000.' Warren replied, 'If you are not disciplined in the little things, you won't be disciplined in the big things.'"

## 3. Buffett's Description in "The Superinvestors of Graham-and-Doddsville"

### 3.1 The Original Article

- **Type**: Essay by Warren Buffett
- **Date**: Fall 1984, published in *Hermes*, Columbia Business School magazine (based on a May 17, 1984 speech)
- **Status**: HIGH — Primary source by Buffett about Schloss
- **Source URL**: https://www8.gsb.columbia.edu/sites/valueinvesting/files/files/Buffett1984.pdf (original)
- **Wikipedia entry**: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Superinvestors_of_Graham-and-Doddsville

**Buffett's description of Schloss** (verbatim from the article):

> "He knows how to identify securities that sell at considerably less than their value to a private owner. And that's all he does... He owns many more stocks than I do and is far less interested in the underlying nature of the business; I don't seem to have very much influence on Walter. That is one of his strengths; no one has much influence on him."

**Performance data Buffett published**:

| Fund | Period | Fund Return (Gross) | Fund Return (Net to LPs) | Market Return |
|------|--------|--------------------|------------------------|---------------|
| WJS Limited Partners (Walter J. Schloss) | 1956–1984 | 21.3% | 16.1% | 8.4% (S&P) |

### 3.2 Buffett's 2006 Letter to Berkshire Hathaway Shareholders

- **Type**: Annual letter
- **Date**: 2006
- **Status**: HIGH — Primary source
- **Source URL**: https://www.berkshirehathaway.com/letters/2006ltr.pdf

**Buffett's description** (from walterschloss.com):
> "Let me end this section by telling you about one of the good guys of Wall Street, my long-time friend Walter Schloss, who last year turned 90. From 1956 to 2002, Walter managed a remarkably successful investment partnership, from which he took not a dime unless his investors made money."

### 3.3 Buffett's Statement Upon Schloss's Death (2012)

- **Type**: Press statement
- **Date**: February 2012
- **Status**: HIGH
- **Source URL**: Reported in Bloomberg, NYT, etc.

> "He had an extraordinary investment record, but even more important, he set an example for integrity in investment management. Walter never made a dime off of his investors unless they themselves made significant money. He charged no fixed fee at all and merely shared in their profits. His fiduciary sense was every bit the equal of his investment skills."

## 5. The 16 Golden Rules (Systematic Investment Framework)

The most systematic statement of Schloss's investment philosophy is the typewritten document "Factors Needed to Make Money in the Stock Market" (March 10, 1994). Here is the complete framework:

### Non-Finance Rules (Psychology & Temperament):

| # | Rule | Category |
|---|------|----------|
| 4 | **Have patience.** Stocks don't go up immediately. | Temperament |
| 5 | **Don't buy on tips or for a quick move.** Let the professionals do that, if they can. Don't sell on bad news. | Discipline |
| 6 | **Don't be afraid to be a loner** but be sure that you are correct in your judgment. You can't be 100% certain but try to look for weaknesses in your thinking. Buy on a scale and sell on a scale up. | Contrarianism |
| 7 | **Have the courage of your convictions** once you have made a decision. | Conviction |
| 8 | **Have a philosophy of investment** and try to follow it. | System |
| 12 | **Listen to suggestions from people you respect.** This doesn't mean you have to accept them. Remember it's your money and generally it is harder to keep money than to make it. Once you lose a lot of money it is hard to make it back. | Humility |
| 13 | **Try not to let your emotions affect your judgment.** Fear and greed are probably the worst emotions to have in connection with the purchase and sale of stocks. | Emotional Control |

### Finance Rules (Quantitative & Analytical):

| # | Rule | Category |
|---|------|----------|
| 1 | **Price is the most important factor** to use in relation to value. | Valuation |
| 2 | **Try to establish the value of the company.** Remember that a share of stock represents a part of a business and is not just a piece of paper. | Valuation |
| 3 | **Use book value as a starting point** to try and establish the value of the enterprise. Be sure that debt does not equal 100% of the equity. (Capital and surplus for the common stock.) | Valuation |
| 9 | **Don't be in too much of a hurry to sell.** If the stock reaches a price that you think is a fair one, then you can sell but often because a stock goes up say 50%, people say sell it and button up your profit. Before selling try to reevaluate the company again and see where the stock sells in relation to its book value. Be aware of the level of the stock market. | Selling |
| 10 | **When buying a stock, I find it helpful to buy near the low of the past few years.** A stock may go as high as 125 and then decline to 60 and you think it attractive. 3 years before the stock sold at 20 which shows that there is some vulnerability in it. | Entry |
| 11 | **Try to buy assets at a discount rather than to buy earnings.** Earnings can change dramatically in a short time. Usually assets change slowly. One has to know much more about a company if one buys earnings. | Asset Preference |
| 14 | **Remember the word compounding.** For example, if you can make 12% a year and reinvest the money back, you will double your money in 6 years, taxes excluded. Remember the rule of 72. Your rate of return into 72 will tell you the number of years to double your money. | Math |
| 15 | **Prefer stocks over bonds.** Bonds will limit your gains and inflation will reduce your purchasing power. | Asset Allocation |
| 16 | **Be careful of leverage.** It can go against you. | Risk |

## 6. Core Beliefs That Appear 3+ Times Across Sources

The following are themes that recur across at least 3 independent sources (interviews, writings, or third-party descriptions). These are Schloss's true beliefs.

### 6.1 Price Is Everything; Value First
- **Sources**: Rule #1; Buffett's description ("if a business is worth a dollar and I can buy it for 40 cents"); OID interview; 2008 Ivey lecture; Forbes 1973
- **Count**: 5+
- **His formulation**: "We buy cheap stocks. That's all we do."

### 6.2 Buy Assets, Not Earnings
- **Sources**: Rule #11; OID interview; AAII article; Forbes 1973; 16 Rules
- **Count**: 5
- **Rationale**: "Earnings can change dramatically in a short time. Usually assets change slowly."
- **Corollary**: Use book value as starting point; prefer price-to-book as metric

### 6.3 Have the Guts to Buy More When Prices Fall
- **Sources**: Grant's conference 1998; OID interview; 2008 Ivey lecture; macro-ops analysis
- **Count**: 4
- **His formulation**: "Basically, we like to buy stocks which we feel are undervalued, and then we have to have the guts to buy more when they go down."

### 6.4 Avoid / Hate Debt and Leverage
- **Sources**: Rule #16; THM Capital interview notes (said "I don't like debt" more than 20 times in one interview); 2008 Ivey lecture; OID interview
- **Count**: 4+

### 6.5 Patience / Time Horizon
- **Sources**: Rule #4; OID interview; 2008 Ivey lecture; compound interest discussion
- **Count**: 4

### 6.6 Diversification (Many Stocks)
- **Sources**: Buffett's description ("He owns many more stocks than I do"); Buffett 2006 letter ("invested in about 1,000 securities"); multiple secondary sources (50-100 holdings)
- **Count**: 3

### 6.7 Don't Try to Predict the Market or Economy
- **Sources**: Forbes 2008 ("What's ahead for the economy? He doesn't worry about it."); Buffett's description ("doesn't worry about whether it's January... whether it's Monday... whether it's an election year"); 16 Rules
- **Count**: 3

### 6.8 Don't Talk to Management / Rely Only on Public Information
- **Sources**: Adam Smith's *Supermoney*; Rule #6; OID interview; 2008 Ivey lecture
- **Count**: 4
- **Key quote**: "I don't go to many meetings. I find reading the reports is usually more informative than talking to management."

### 6.9 Never Sell on Bad News
- **Sources**: Rule #5; Daniel MNKE analysis; OID interview
- **Count**: 3

### 6.10 Fear and Greed Are the Worst Emotions
- **Sources**: Rule #13; "Benjamin Graham and Security Analysis: A Reminiscence"; OID interview; 2008 Ivey lecture
- **Count**: 4

### 6.11 Simplicity of Approach
- **Sources**: All interviews and articles consistently note his method was simple; OID interview ("We're buying in a way that we don't have to be too smart about the business"); Grant's conference ("That's really the history of Ben Graham. That's it.")
- **Count**: 5+

### 6.12 Frugality / Low Costs
- **Sources**: 2008 Ivey lecture (family history of bankruptcy); office expense of ~$11,000/year; no employees; operated from small room in Tweedy Browne's office
- **Count**: 3

## 7. Book Recommendations & Intellectual Lineage

Schloss was not known for producing reading lists, but his intellectual lineage is clearly documented:

### 7.1 Directly Stated Influences:

1. **Benjamin Graham, *Security Analysis*** (1934)
   - The foundational text. Partner Armand Erpf told him in 1935: "Read the book and when you know everything in it, you won't have to read anything else."
   - Source: Schloss's own reminiscence

2. **Benjamin Graham, *The Intelligent Investor*** (1949)
   - Schloss notes it was "a book for the layman but it focused on security analysis and gave prestige to the field."
   - Source: Schloss's own reminiscence

3. **Value Line Investment Survey**
   - His primary research tool. He did not use computers; he relied on Value Line and Moody's manuals.
   - Source: Multiple sources including AAII, Buffett's description, Adam Smith

4. **Moody's Investment Manuals**
   - Used for balance sheet data. Recommended looking at Graham-Newman's holdings in 1947-1956 Moody's manuals.
   - Source: Schloss's own reminiscence

### 7.2 Indirect Intellectual Lineage (Inferred from His Method):

- **Benjamin Graham** — direct teacher and employer (9+ years)
- **Jerome Newman** — partner of Graham, co-manager of Graham-Newman
- **David Dodd** — co-author of *Security Analysis*, acknowledged by Schloss
- **Philip Fisher** — a "Dozen Things" article links Schloss and Fisher (but Schloss differed: he preferred quantitative over qualitative)

### 7.3 Books About or Featuring Schloss:

1. **Adam Smith (George Goodman), *Supermoney* (1972)** — features Schloss prominently
2. **Bruce Greenwald et al., *Value Investing: From Graham to Buffett and Beyond* (2001)** — contains a chapter/profile of Schloss
3. **Alice Schroeder, *The Snowball: Warren Buffett and the Business of Life* (2008)** — contains biographical details about Schloss's family background

## 8. Contradictions and Tensions in the Record

The following tensions exist across the source base. Recorded as-is, without reconciliation:

### 8.1 Diversification vs. Conviction
- Schloss preached diversification (50-100 stocks)
- But also said "Have the courage of your convictions" (Rule #7) and "Buy on a scale"
- **Tension**: How does one have high conviction while holding 100 stocks?

### 8.2 Partnership Returns: Multiple Different Numbers Circulated
- Buffett's 1984 article: 21.3% gross / 16.1% net (1956-1984)
- OID interview (1989): 21.6% (for 33 years ended 12/31/88)
- Wikipedia: 15.3% compound return over 4.5 decades
- 2008 Ivey transcript: 15.3% vs. S&P 11.5% (1956-2000)
- Macro Ops: 16.4% annually for 32 years vs. S&P 9.8%
- **Note**: These differ because of time periods measured (gross vs. net, different end dates). No single definitive number exists.

### 8.3 "Never Sold on Bad News" vs. Active Selling
- Rule #5 says "Don't sell on bad news"
- But OID interview discusses their process for selling when stocks reach fair value
- **Tension**: When and how to sell is not clearly resolved in the record

### 8.4 Avoided Management vs. Held Management Accountable
- Schloss famously did NOT talk to management
- But one of his criteria was "management that owns a lot of stock" and "honest management"
- **Tension**: How do you assess management quality without talking to them?

### 8.5 Graham's Conservatism vs. Missing Opportunities
- In the OID interview, Schloss noted that Graham's Depression-era caution caused him to miss opportunities:
> "In the process of avoiding it, he missed a lot of opportunities. That's one of the problems you always have - you don't really lose, but you don't really make, either."
- Schloss acknowledged this trade-off explicitly, suggesting he was aware his own approach might have the same limitation

### 8.6 Office Location
- Some sources say he ran Walter J. Schloss Associates from a small room inside Tweedy, Browne's offices
- Other sources say he worked at 230 Park Avenue
- **Resolution**: He likely started in Tweedy Browne's space and later moved; the record is unclear on timing
